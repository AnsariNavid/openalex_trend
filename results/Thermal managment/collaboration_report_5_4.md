## Collaboration Directions by Top German Partners

### Technical University of Munich
This collaboration is centered on turning large-scale AI and high-performance computing into practical engines for biological discovery and autonomous systems. The abstracts point to joint work on foundation models for proteins, DNA, and viral genomes that aims to predict structure, function, phenotypes, and emerging variants without relying on expensive handcrafted pipelines, while also making these models scalable on supercomputers. A second direction is safety-critical autonomy: integrating learned dynamics into real-time control, forecasting human motion for robot interaction, and using foundation models to generate and analyze rare driving scenarios so autonomous systems can be tested against edge cases before deployment. In medical imaging, the partnership also pushes benchmark-driven methods that make segmentation and registration more reliable and generalizable across sites, with uncertainty estimates designed to support clinical review rather than just maximize leaderboard scores. 
### Heidelberg University
The collaboration with Heidelberg University is focused on building evaluation frameworks where AI methods can be trusted on harder, more realistic biomedical tasks. One strand targets full-text chemical entity recognition and indexing, aiming to extract and normalize chemical knowledge from the literature so biomedical search and downstream discovery are less bottlenecked by manual curation. Another strand uses community challenges to test whether diffusion MRI signal models actually transfer across acquisition settings and tissue types, addressing a core gap between methods that work on narrow datasets and methods that remain useful in practice. The partnership also reaches toward generative vision systems that learn object detectors without labels by coupling image synthesis and detection, reducing dependence on expensive annotation. 
### Technical University of Darmstadt
This collaboration is oriented toward closing the gap between simulation-trained intelligence and dependable real-world control. The abstracts emphasize methods that make simulators more useful for robot learning, including domain randomization and robust value-iteration schemes that produce policies able to tolerate model mismatch and physical perturbations after transfer to hardware. A complementary direction is using generative and foundation models to create richer autonomous-driving scenarios, so validation is not limited to scripted or overly narrow test cases. There is also a systems-level thread around simulation and AI for energy and industrial infrastructure, where the goal is to improve how complex physical processes are designed, validated, and operated under real constraints. 
### University of Bremen
The collaboration with the University of Bremen is aimed at making Earth-system digital twins more actionable and scientifically credible. One direction is to build interactive Earth representations that do not just simulate physics, but also account for human interventions, responses, and governance, so the resulting systems can support policy and decision-making rather than only produce forecasts. Another direction tackles a specific bottleneck in climate modeling: replacing costly and biased subgrid parameterizations with machine learning models that are causally informed or uncertainty-aware, in order to preserve physical realism, improve stability, and better capture extremes. Together, these efforts are trying to build climate intelligence systems that are both computationally tractable and trustworthy enough for real-world use. 
### Johannes Gutenberg University Mainz
This partnership is strongly shaped by algorithmic acceleration for data-intensive science. In bioinformatics and genomics, the collaboration develops GPU-native methods for protein search and Pair-HMM alignment so rapidly growing sequence databases and variant-calling workloads can be processed fast enough to keep pace with modern biology pipelines. In astroparticle physics, the work optimizes photon propagation simulations for IceCube by redesigning GPU implementations around detector geometry and collision handling, reducing the cost of modeling detector response at scale. A separate physics direction applies advanced lattice-QCD operator construction to resolve disputed claims about two-nucleon bound states, showing a collaboration that combines numerical method design with high-stakes scientific validation. 
### University Hospital Heidelberg
The collaboration with University Hospital Heidelberg is concentrated on stress-testing quantitative MRI models under realistic variation rather than idealized settings. Through the MEMENTO challenge, the joint work asks which diffusion MRI signal representations remain accurate when encoding schemes, diffusion timings, and tissue contexts change, instead of assuming that success on one protocol generalizes automatically. The practical problem being addressed is model selection for clinical and research imaging pipelines: identifying approaches that fail gracefully and expose their limits before they are used in broader studies. 
### German Cancer Research Center
This collaboration is pursuing a highly integrative modeling agenda through the “Virtual Child.” The aim is to build a cell-level computational model of normal and cancerous development that can be used to run virtual clinical trials, narrowing the search space for pediatric cancer treatments before they reach patients. Rather than describing tumors retrospectively, the partnership is trying to create a predictive system that can prioritize interventions and expose developmental mechanisms that conventional experiments cannot explore at comparable scale. 
### Universität Hamburg
The collaboration with Universität Hamburg is directed toward data infrastructures and computational frameworks for Earth-scale decision support. The Earth Virtualization Engine work aims to combine high-resolution physical models, machine learning, and federated access to exabyte-scale climate data so users can interrogate scenarios interactively instead of relying on static outputs. Another concrete direction is benchmark-building in medical image registration, where the goal is to widen task diversity and evaluation rigor so progress reflects clinically relevant robustness across modalities and problem settings. The partnership also touches applied remote sensing, using automated multi-band extraction methods to derive bathymetry and shorelines with minimal manual intervention. 
### Klinikum rechts der Isar
This collaboration combines clinically grounded mechanism studies with targeted medical imaging methods. In subarachnoid hemorrhage, the joint work is trying to clarify what actually drives perfusion deficits and microvasospasm formation, refining therapeutic understanding by separating plausible mechanisms from those not supported by evidence. On the imaging side, the partnership develops anatomy-aware segmentation strategies for difficult tubular structures such as the dilated pancreatic duct, aiming to make models focus on the right region and geometry rather than miss clinically important fine structures. It also contributes to broader registration benchmarking efforts that expand evaluation to more complex and multimodal clinical scenarios. 
### Forschungszentrum Jülich
The collaboration with Forschungszentrum Jülich is geared toward making machine learning and extreme-scale computation usable for scientifically demanding environments. In atmospheric science, the abstracts point to joint work on benchmark design so ML methods can be compared on shared, well-posed problems instead of fragmented datasets and inconsistent evaluation setups. Another direction addresses resilience in exascale simulation, asking how large computations can continue producing valid results despite hardware faults, bit flips, and prohibitive checkpoint costs. Alongside this, the partnership contributes precision lattice-QCD calculations that serve as numerical benchmarks for particle and neutrino physics, linking algorithmic rigor to downstream experimental interpretation.

## Theme Summaries from Abstracts

### Computer science
These abstracts frame collaboration as integration across advanced computing domains, especially where quantum computing, AI, networking, manufacturing, and medical intervention systems meet. The shared emphasis is on combining hardware, software, algorithms, and domain expertise to build scalable, data-driven systems while addressing deployment complexity, interoperability, and cross-disciplinary barriers.

### Artificial intelligence
The abstracts show AI collaboration centered on high-impact, data-intensive applications such as healthcare prediction and imaging, drug discovery, environmental science, biomolecular design, and next-generation wireless systems. A recurring theme is federated, distributed, or large-scale infrastructure that enables robust models to generalize across heterogeneous data, institutions, and real-time operational settings.

### Machine learning
These works highlight machine learning partnerships that connect statistical modeling with scientific simulation, control, and physical knowledge in climate science, molecular dynamics, PDE solving, and network optimization. The common goal is to make ML more reliable and useful in real-world systems through physics-informed methods, efficient software frameworks, and real-time deployment on embedded or high-performance platforms.

### Computer vision
The abstracts portray computer vision collaboration as a blend of core methodological advances and applied sensing problems, spanning tensor methods, segmentation, tracking, robotics, remote sensing, and hazard monitoring. Across domains, researchers pair visual data pipelines with deep models to improve recognition, prediction, and benchmarking in settings where robustness, structured representations, and practical evaluation matter.

### Deep learning
These abstracts emphasize deep learning as a shared foundation for medical imaging, report generation, robotics, visual representation, chip design, and foundation-model-based autonomy. Collaboration focuses on pushing models from benchmark success toward broader utility through better generalization across domains, integration with structured knowledge, acceleration on modern hardware, and adaptation to safety-critical workflows.

The collaboration theme is using ANN architectures as flexible surrogates for complex physical processes while improving training tools, computational efficiency, and deployment in domain-specific simulation environments.

## 1) Narrative Analysis

The collaboration profile around **Nvidia (United States)** and **Nvidia (United Kingdom)** is large and technically concentrated, with **1,600 publications** overall. The strongest thematic base is in **computer science**, especially **artificial intelligence**, **machine learning**, **computer vision**, **deep learning**, and **parallel computing**. A smaller but notable strand connects these capabilities to **medicine**, including imaging and segmentation-related work. German collaboration intensity is led by a small set of repeat partners. The most active institution is the **Technical University of Munich (28 publications)**, followed by **Heidelberg University (12)** and the **Technical University of Darmstadt (9)**. A second tier includes the **University of Bremen**, **Johannes Gutenberg University Mainz**, **University Hospital Heidelberg**, **German Cancer Research Center**, **Universität Hamburg**, **Klinikum rechts der Isar**, and **Forschungszentrum Jülich**. This mix suggests a portfolio spanning core AI, systems, and clinically adjacent applications. Yearly activity shows a rapid expansion phase followed by consolidation. Output rose from **170 publications in 2020** to **288 in 2022**, while the number of unique German collaborator institutes increased from **11** to **62** over the same period. In **2023**, the network became narrower, with **248 publications** and **25 German institutes**, before broadening again in **2024** to **41 institutes** alongside **265 publications**. **2025** marks the strongest publication year at **338**, though with **33 German institutes**, indicating deeper work with a more selective set of partners.
